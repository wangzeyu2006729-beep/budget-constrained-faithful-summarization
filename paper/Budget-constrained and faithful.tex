\begin{abstract}
We study faithful abstractive summarization under a sentence-level budget by separating neural candidate generation from combinatorial content selection. The system first generates multiple candidate summaries with a pretrained model, deduplicates their sentences into a provenance-preserving pool, and then selects a bounded subset using utility, factuality, and redundancy scores. We audit three selectors: an ILP with a soft pairwise redundancy penalty, an MMR greedy baseline, and a DPP-inspired greedy log-determinant heuristic. Experiments on CNN/DailyMail and Multi-News evaluate ROUGE, BERTScore, and faithfulness metrics where local result files are available. The release emphasizes paper-code traceability and marks unavailable or pending metrics explicitly.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Problem Definition and Framework Overview}

Given an input document or document set $\mathcal{D}$, abstractive summarization aims to produce a concise and faithful textual summary that captures the salient information of $\mathcal{D}$. Most existing approaches formulate this task as conditional sequence generation, which achieves strong fluency but provides limited control over output length and factual consistency.

We instead cast abstractive summarization as a \emph{budget-constrained combinatorial selection problem} over a discrete candidate space. Specifically, we construct a candidate pool of sentences 
$\mathcal{C} = \{c_1, \dots, c_n\}$ 
using a pretrained language model. The objective is to select a subset $S \subseteq \mathcal{C}$ that maximizes summary quality under an explicit budget constraint:
\begin{equation}
\max_{S \subseteq \mathcal{C}} f(S)
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad
\sum_{c_i \in S} l_i \le B,
\end{equation}
where $l_i$ denotes the cost (e.g., length) of candidate $c_i$, and $B$ is the budget.

We design $f(S)$ to capture three key properties of high-quality summaries:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Utility (U):} promotes relevance and semantic coverage of the source document;
    \item \textbf{Factuality (F):} encourages consistency between candidate sentences and $\mathcal{D}$;
    \item \textbf{Redundancy (R):} penalizes semantic overlap among selected candidates to encourage diversity.
\end{itemize}
These components are integrated within a unified combinatorial objective to guide subset selection under the budget constraint.

In the implementation audited for this release, $f(S)$ is operationalized as a sentence-level scoring objective rather than a theoretical monotone submodular objective. Utility and factuality are modular sentence scores, while redundancy is represented by pairwise ROUGE-L overlap and used either as a soft penalty in the ILP objective or as a diversity term in greedy selectors. This formulation enables exact optimization of the implemented ILP instance and diversity-aware heuristic selection, but we do not claim a general submodularity guarantee or exact DPP inference.

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the overall framework, which consists of three stages:
(1) \emph{candidate generation}, where a language model produces a diverse set of candidate sentences;
(2) \emph{combinatorial content planning}, where an optimizer selects a subset of candidates by jointly considering utility, factuality, and redundancy under a budget constraint; and
(3) \emph{constrained summary realization}, where the selected candidates are ordered to produce the final summary.

This formulation offers three key advantages. First, it provides explicit control over the maximum number of selected sentences through the budget constraint used by the selector. Second, it encourages faithfulness by scoring candidate sentences against the source document before final selection. Third, it enables a modular integration of neural generation and combinatorial optimization, allowing pretrained language models to be used without retraining while applying selection-time constraints.

\section{Limitations}
The current system uses a sentence-count budget, not a token-level or word-level budget. The DPP variant is implemented as a DPP-inspired greedy log-determinant selector and should be interpreted as a heuristic rather than exact DPP sampling or inference. Some faithfulness metrics are optional because they require external checkpoints or third-party repositories, and unavailable metrics are marked with dashes in the result table. Several Llama-3 Multi-News CO results were still pending at the time of this release audit and are therefore not reported as completed results.

\section{Conclusion}
We present a release-audited framework for budget-constrained faithful summarization that separates candidate generation from sentence-level combinatorial selection. The implementation supports direct baselines and CO selectors with traceable candidate pools, provenance records, and metric outputs. The release documentation records where the paper, code, and result files agree, and explicitly marks missing or pending evidence rather than filling unsupported scores.
